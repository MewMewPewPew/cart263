### Response: 
> To start this response, I'll explain my experience the content of Project 1 that the group 6 made for this class. </br> </br>
The page opens with a black screen contrasting the white background which has an instruction: "Click me". As we do it, the mouse hovers and changes the luminosity of the black screen to lighter before the counter in the middle adding 1 click. This results in a random color background change with a 15% change for the background to be changed to an array of random images downloaded from the internet. Below the counter is a reset button that brings us back to the start as anticipated. As we click more, each multiple of 5 click reached makes an image appear as a form of a card, with a random angle and position on the page. When reaching 30 clicks, an alerting message transfers: "YOU ARE BREAKING THE SYSTEM", accompanied by a shaking of the screen to let us know that we shouldn't listen to the first instruction and should stop clicking, if we care about the "system". When reaching 50 clicks, an alert event listener lets us know that the "System Overload! Resetting...", by pressing "ok", the page is back to its beginning.
</br> </br>
I personally really love the concept and thought behind the game "Cookie-Clicker", which was an inspiration for this website. The aspect of that game where you are prompted to "click" more and always more "productively", even if those clicks ultimately reflect the capitalistic greed of collecting more capital/currency/bonus/stuff leading to the literal destruction of the earth and our universe. I feel the essence of collecting images and clicks is followed in group6's project-1, with the same idea of tension between our greed or simple curiosity with clicking and collecting and the consequence of our action breaking the system is what I find most interesting. Perhaps the revolt and breaking of the system actually enable some people to click more. I was surprised by the first 30-clicks alert because of its unprompted nature. The cards random angle and position was the most compelling for me because it made it more "real" or chaotic with a personal experience delivered through randomness. I wish there was more pictures all together for the immersion of randomness and unlimited overwhelming of information the internet can represent. Although I think they wanted to show free, random visuals one would see when surfing the mainstream web, as an artist, I would have loved to see a linked aesthetic, or maybe a way to alternate the images. For an idea more complex and totally not expected for this kind of project, I would be interesting to add ways to unlock clicks automations as/or rewards, and afk(away from keyboard) features/automation (like in Cookie-Clicker) to develop this even more, or starting to propose the "same kind of" image like our social media algorithm trains us to do. In the group6's presentation, they mentioned wanting to make it accessible mobile which I think is a great thing to do for accessibility. Furthermore, they explain how the clicking action of the website simulation how such a simple action can lead to addition, perhaps because of its simplicity that comes with rewards such as bright colors and pictures human might like to see. The group6's project one might look like a simple game in appears, but it actually brings deeper question about our digital habits, productivity, greed and our consumption of "free" images on the web.
